Outline

Our goal was to use CO2 emissions data, reported from 1850 to 2020, to predict future emissions until 2050 globally - the benchmark year for reducing emissions targets as established by the United Nations. Then, with the help of a machine machine learning model, we can test our hypothesis.

  • Mine and clean the datasets
  • Establish a working database with pgadmin/postgreSQL
  • Develop a machine learning model to forecast emissions data until 2050, thereby testing the hypothesis with the efficacy of the model
  • Develop interactive maps to allow users to view current and past CO2 emissions globally to understand these trends.

Synopsis

CO2 emissions are the primary cause of global warming and it continues to drive devastating climate change and natural disasters. CO2 has the ability to do this because it is a heat-trapping molecule. Thus, an abundance of CO2 elevates global surface temperature which results in visible climate change to the human eye such as coral reef destruction through ocean acidification and an unusally high amount of forest fires. We want to find out what the CO2 emissions trends are on different continents currently and in the future.

The Big Questions

We have several questions we expect that this data will answer. The main questions we want to answer are:

 

1) Can we use machine learning to accurately predict CO2 emissions between now and 2050?

 

2) Will CO2 emissions continue to rise within the highest-emitting countries between now and 2050?

 

3) How will the projected CO2 emissions affect surface temperatures?

The Data

Our team selected world emissions data from Our World in Data's Github page. Our World in Data is an open source website dedicated to monitoring greenhouse gas emissions by country. They compile data from a variety of national and international sources on their website. We also used data from Berkeley Earth which provided us Earth surface temperature data and data from Natural Earth for each country's GeoJSON data.
